## Page 16 — Multiples (Number & Pattern)

### Concepts
- Understanding what a multiple of a number is
- Identifying multiples of specific numbers (3, 6, 12)
- Recognising the relationship between multiples of related numbers (e.g., multiples of 6 are also multiples of 3)
- Determining whether a given number is a multiple of another number, with explanation
- Finding the nth multiple of a number (e.g., the 2nd multiple, 3rd multiple, 10th multiple)
- Understanding that a multiple of a number is obtained by multiplying that number by a whole number

### Key Rules
- A number is a **multiple** of another number if it can be divided exactly (no remainder) by that number.
- 12 is a multiple of 6 because 12 = 2 x 6, and also a multiple of 6 because 12 = 6 + 6.
- 18 is also a multiple of 6 because 18 = 3 x 6.
- All multiples of 6 are also multiples of 3 (because 6 itself is a multiple of 3).
- A multiple of 12 is always also a multiple of 6 **and** a multiple of 3.
- 10 is **not** a multiple of 4 because you cannot multiply 4 by a whole number to get 10 (4 x 2 = 8, 4 x 3 = 12, so 10 falls in between).
- The **nth multiple** of a number = n x that number (e.g., the 10th multiple of 3 = 10 x 3 = 30).

### Areas to Focus On
- **Understand the relationship between multiples of related numbers:** If a is a multiple of b, then every multiple of a is also a multiple of b. For example, every multiple of 6 is also a multiple of 3, and every multiple of 12 is also a multiple of 6 and of 3.
- **Practice explaining WHY a number is or is not a multiple:** When asked to explain, always show the multiplication (e.g., "68 is a multiple of 17 because 17 x 4 = 68") or show that the number falls between two consecutive multiples (e.g., "27 is not a multiple of 4 because 4 x 6 = 24 and 4 x 7 = 28, and 27 is between them").
- **Finding the nth multiple:** The nth multiple of any number is simply n x that number. For example, the 10th multiple of 227 = 10 x 227 = 2 270.
- **Listing multiples systematically:** When writing out all multiples less than a certain number, use skip-counting (add the number repeatedly) and be careful not to miss any or go past the limit.
- **Be careful with large numbers:** Questions like "Is 600 a multiple of 50?" require confident division or multiplication with larger values. Practice these to build fluency.
