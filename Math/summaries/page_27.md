## Page 27 — Sharing Equally / Equivalent Fractions & Comparing Fractions (Number & Pattern)

### Concepts
- Dividing a whole (a chocolate slab of 12 blocks) equally among different numbers of people
- Finding all factors of 12 as the number of people who can share equally
- Expressing each person's share as a fraction of the whole
- Understanding equivalent fractions (e.g., 1/3 = 4/12)
- Comparing fractions using visual ribbon models (greater than, less than, or equal to)
- Using fraction strips/ribbons to decide which fraction is bigger

### Key Rules
- When sharing equally, the number of people must be a factor of the total (factors of 12: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12)
- Fraction per person = number of blocks per person / total blocks (e.g., 4 blocks out of 12 = 4/12 = 1/3)
- Equivalent fractions represent the same amount (e.g., 1/3 = 4/12 because 4 x 3 = 12)
- To compare fractions, you can use visual models (ribbon strips), common denominators, or cross-multiplication
- A fraction with a smaller denominator (and same numerator) is larger (e.g., 1/3 > 1/8)
- When two fractions are equivalent, use the equals sign (e.g., 1/2 = 5/10)

### Areas to Focus On
- **Factors and divisibility:** Know all the factors of 12 (1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12) — this is the foundation for understanding equal sharing
- **Equivalent fractions:** Practice simplifying fractions and recognising when two fractions are equal (e.g., 4/12 = 1/3). Multiply or divide both numerator and denominator by the same number
- **Comparing unit fractions:** When the numerator is 1, the fraction with the smaller denominator is the larger fraction (e.g., 1/3 > 1/8 because thirds are bigger pieces than eighths)
- **Comparing non-unit fractions:** Convert to common denominators or use benchmark fractions (like 1/2) to compare. For example, 2/5 < 1/2 because 2/5 = 4/10 and 1/2 = 5/10
- **Visual models:** Use fraction strips or ribbon diagrams to build intuition about fraction size — being comfortable with these models helps check answers
- **Systematic investigation:** When asked to find "all possible solutions," work through numbers in order and check each one to ensure nothing is missed
- **Explaining reasoning:** Practice writing clear explanations (like explaining why two fractions are equivalent) — this is a common question style in assessments
