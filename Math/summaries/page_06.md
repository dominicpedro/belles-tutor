## Page 6 — Adding by Breaking Up Numbers (Number & Pattern — Addition)

### Concepts
- Adding decimal numbers using a "breaking up" strategy (decomposition)
- Breaking a number into its whole number and decimal parts before adding
- Adding distances (kilometres) in real-world word problems
- Mental maths strategies for addition of decimals

### Key Rules
- **Breaking Up Strategy:** To add two decimal numbers, break one number into convenient parts, then add each part step-by-step.
  - Example from the page: 2,1 + 0,5 = ?
    - Break 0,5 into 0,3 and 0,2 (or into parts that make the addition easier)
    - Alternatively: break up 3,1 into 3 and 0,1
    - Then: 2,1 + 3 = 5,1 and 5,1 + 0,5 = 5,6 (total = 5,6 km)
  - The comma is used as the decimal separator (South African convention), so 2,1 means 2.1.
- **Number line jumps:** You can visualise the breaking-up strategy as jumps on a number line — first jump the whole number, then jump the decimal part.

### Areas to Focus On
- **Practice decomposing numbers:** Get comfortable breaking a decimal number into its whole-number part and decimal part (e.g., 6,7 = 6 + 0,7)
- **Add in steps:** Always add the whole number part first, then the decimal part — this reduces errors
- **Watch the decimal comma:** In South African notation, a comma is used instead of a decimal point (2,3 means two-and-three-tenths)
- **Check by estimating:** Round each number to the nearest whole number first to get an estimate, then do the breaking-up strategy to get the exact answer
- **Multi-step word problems:** In Question 3, three numbers must be added — practise chaining additions (add two numbers first, then add the third to that result)
- **Number chains (Q4):** Make sure to add carefully step-by-step; each new value depends on the previous one being correct, so one mistake cascades through the whole chain
