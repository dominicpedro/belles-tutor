### Concepts
- Counting in multiples of 15 and 1 500

- Relationship between multiplication and multiples
- Applying multiplication to real-world money problems (cost of items, savings, budgeting)
- Division as the inverse of multiplication (how many items can you buy with a given amount)

---

### Key Rules
- The **nth multiple** of a number is found by multiplying: nth multiple = n x the number
  - e.g. the 7th multiple of 15 = 7 x 15 = 105
- To find **how many items** you can buy: Total money / Price per item = Number of items
- To find **total cost**: Number of items x Price per item = Total cost
- To find **total savings over time**: Amount saved per month x Number of months = Total saved

### Areas to Focus On

- **Calculating nth multiples:** Practice finding the nth multiple by multiplying n x the base number. Belle should drill: "the 7th multiple of 15 means 7 x 15." Question 2c was answered as 756 instead of 750, suggesting a small arithmetic slip.
- **Division for "how many can you buy" problems:** Question 6 answers were significantly off. Belle needs focused practice on dividing larger amounts by 1 500. A helpful strategy: think of R1 500 as "one and a half thousand" — so R3 000 buys 2, R6 000 buys 4, R9 000 buys 6, R12 000 buys 8, etc.
- **Distinguishing multiplication from division in word problems:** Knowing when to multiply (find total cost) vs. divide (find how many you can buy) is critical. Practice identifying the operation from the question wording.
- **Checking reasonableness of answers:** For Q6a, if each ticket is R1 500, then 18 tickets would cost R27 000 — far more than R10 000. Encourage Belle to do a quick check: "Does my answer make sense?"
- **Savings calculations (Q5):** The answer of R1 500 for 7 months of R150/month savings suggests Belle may have misread the question or multiplied by 10 instead of 7. Reinforce reading the question carefully and writing out the multiplication.
- **Counting sequences:** The counting in 15s and 1 500s was mostly correct, showing solid understanding of skip-counting patterns. Keep reinforcing this as a foundation for multiplication fluency.
