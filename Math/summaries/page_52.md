## Page 52 — Determine How Much Time Has Passed Between (Measurement: Time)

### Concepts
- Calculating elapsed time between two given times (hours, minutes, and seconds)
- Using a time line to visualise and work out elapsed time
- Comparing durations and finding the difference between them
- Working with time expressed in hours:minutes:seconds format (e.g., 01:30:48)
- Understanding elapsed time across am/pm boundaries

### Key Rules
- To find elapsed time, count forward from the start time to the end time
- A useful strategy is to break the counting into chunks: first count to the nearest full hour, then count the remaining minutes (and seconds)
- When comparing durations, subtract the shorter duration from the longer duration to find the difference
- 1 hour = 60 minutes; 1 minute = 60 seconds
- When times are given in hh:mm:ss format, subtract each component carefully, borrowing where necessary

### Areas to Focus On
- **Breaking elapsed time into manageable chunks:** Practice counting from the start time to the nearest whole hour, then adding the remaining minutes. Draw time lines to make this visual
- **Crossing noon (am to pm) and midnight:** Pay special attention when start time is am and end time is pm (or when crossing midnight with 24-hour times). Count the hours carefully
- **24-hour time conversions:** Make sure to understand that 14:00 = 2:00 pm, 21:00 = 9:00 pm, etc., and that going from 21:00 to 08:30 means crossing midnight (next day)
- **Subtraction with borrowing in time:** When subtracting seconds or minutes, if the number being subtracted is larger, borrow 1 minute (= 60 seconds) or 1 hour (= 60 minutes). For example, 3 min 10 sec - 2 min 45 sec requires borrowing: convert to 2 min 70 sec - 2 min 45 sec = 0 min 25 sec
- **Direction of subtraction:** Always subtract the smaller time from the larger time; do not reverse the order of subtraction for individual components
- **Double-checking with conversion to a single unit:** When confused, convert everything to seconds (or minutes) first, do the subtraction, then convert back. This avoids borrowing errors
