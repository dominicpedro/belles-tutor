## Page 8 — Addition Strategies: Breaking Up & Adding Left to Right (Number & Pattern)

### Concepts
- Adding two-digit and three-digit numbers using mental math strategies
- "Breaking up" both numbers into tens and units (e.g., 235 + 57 = 200 + 30 + 5 + 50 + 7 = 280 + 12 = 292)
- "Adding from left to right" strategy (add hundreds first, then tens, then units)
- Solving word problems that require addition
- Applying different strategies to the same calculation and comparing methods

### Key Rules
- **Breaking up strategy**: Split both numbers into place values (hundreds, tens, units), add each group, then combine the totals.
  - Example: 235 + 57 → 200 + 30 + 50 + 5 + 7 = 280 + 12 = 292
- **Adding from left to right**: Start adding from the largest place value and work toward the smallest.
  - Example: 245 + 33 = 278 (add 200 + 40 + 30 + 5 + 3, working left to right)
- Both strategies should give the same answer — use one to check the other.

### Areas to Focus On
- Practice breaking numbers into hundreds, tens, and units quickly — this is the foundation of mental addition
- When adding three numbers, try grouping pairs that make "friendly" numbers first (e.g., numbers that add to 10, 50, or 100)
- Always estimate first to check if your answer is reasonable (e.g., 235 + 57 is roughly 240 + 60 = 300, so 292 makes sense)
- For Question 4, learn at least three strategies: (1) breaking up, (2) adding left to right, (3) rounding and compensating (e.g., 35 + 39 = 35 + 40 - 1 = 74)
- Pay close attention to carrying over when units add up to more than 10 (e.g., 53 + 38: 3 + 8 = 11, carry the 1)
- Word problems require reading carefully to identify which operation to use — here, "how much money does she need" signals addition
