## Page 48 — Lines of Symmetry and Diagonals of Rectangles (Space & Shape / Geometry)

### Concepts
- Understanding **line symmetry**: if you fold a shape along a line so that one half lies exactly on top of the other, the shape has line symmetry
- The fold line is called the **line of symmetry**
- Understanding **diagonals**: a diagonal is a line segment that joins two vertices of a shape which are not already joined by the edges of the shape
- Determining whether the diagonals of a rectangle are also lines of symmetry
- Identifying lines of symmetry in rectangles by cutting, folding, and checking

### Key Rules
- **Line of symmetry**: A line that divides a shape into two identical halves that fold exactly onto each other.
- **Diagonal**: A line segment connecting two non-adjacent vertices (corners) of a shape.
- A rectangle has **2 lines of symmetry** (one vertical, one horizontal through the midpoints of opposite sides).
- The **diagonals of a rectangle are NOT lines of symmetry** -- folding along a diagonal does not produce two halves that match exactly (unless the rectangle is a square).
- A square (special rectangle) has **4 lines of symmetry** (2 through midpoints of sides + 2 along the diagonals).

### Areas to Focus On
- **Distinguish between diagonals and lines of symmetry**: Many students mistakenly think a rectangle's diagonals are lines of symmetry. Practise folding to prove they are not.
- **Hands-on folding**: Physically cutting out shapes and folding them is the best way to verify lines of symmetry. If both halves match perfectly, it is a line of symmetry.
- **Know the count**: Memorise that a general rectangle always has exactly 2 lines of symmetry. A square (a special rectangle) has 4.
- **Vocabulary precision**: Be clear on the definitions of "line of symmetry" vs. "diagonal" -- they are different concepts and a diagonal is only a line of symmetry in special shapes (e.g., square, rhombus).
