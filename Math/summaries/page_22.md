## Page 22 — Fractions on a Number Line / Fractions of Money (Number & Pattern)

### Concepts
- Placing and reading fractions on a number line (fifths and thirds)
- Converting between mixed numbers, improper fractions, and positions on a number line
- Calculating a fraction of an amount of money (South African Rand)
- Understanding saving as a fraction of earnings
- Recognising South African currency (banknotes and coins shown: R10 note, R20 note, coins)
- Finding unit fractions and non-unit fractions of whole-number Rand amounts

### Key Rules
- To place fractions on a number line, divide each whole-number interval into equal parts matching the denominator
- Equivalent fractions occupy the same point on a number line
- To find a fraction of an amount: multiply the amount by the numerator and divide by the denominator (or divide first, then multiply)
- Example: 1/3 of R18 = R18 ÷ 3 = R6
- Example: 3/5 of R30 = (R30 ÷ 5) × 3 = R18
- Saving a fraction of earnings means setting aside that fraction of the total earned

### Areas to Focus On
- Practice converting between improper fractions and mixed numbers quickly (e.g., 7/5 = 1 2/5)
- When finding a fraction of an amount, always divide by the denominator first, then multiply by the numerator — this avoids large multiplication errors
- Show all working steps clearly: first the division, then the multiplication
- Pay careful attention to the denominator — it tells you how many equal parts to split the amount into
- For non-unit fractions (numerator greater than 1), remember the two-step process: divide then multiply
- Use real-life money examples to build intuition (e.g., "If I have R60 and spend 3/4, how much is left?")
- Double-check by estimating: 3/4 of R60 should be "most of R60" — if your answer is small, something went wrong
- Memorise common fraction-of-amount pairs: 1/5 of R100 = R20, 1/4 of R100 = R25, 1/10 of R100 = R10
