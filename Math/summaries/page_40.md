## Page 40 — Reflections and Patterns (Space & Shape)

### Concepts
- Reflecting (flipping) a shape to create a symmetrical pattern
- Identifying errors in reflected patterns
- Extending a pattern by repeatedly reflecting a shape
- Using a dot grid to accurately draw reflections
- Understanding lines of symmetry in the context of reflections

### Key Rules
- When you **reflect** a shape, it creates a mirror image — the shape flips across a line of reflection.
- A reflected shape is the same size and shape as the original, but reversed (like looking in a mirror).
- When reflecting on a dot grid, each point of the shape must be the same distance from the line of reflection on both sides.
- Repeatedly reflecting a shape creates a repeating symmetrical pattern (like a frieze or border pattern).
- The orientation alternates with each reflection: original, flipped, original, flipped, etc.

### Areas to Focus On
- **Accuracy on dot grids:** Count dots carefully when drawing reflections — each vertex of the shape must be plotted at the correct reflected position.
- **Understanding the line of reflection:** Be clear about where the mirror line is (horizontal, between the shapes). Every point flips across this line.
- **Checking your work:** After reflecting, the original and reflected shape together should look symmetrical — fold the page along the mirror line and the shapes should match exactly.
- **Recognising errors in reflections:** Practice spotting when a shape has been reflected incorrectly (wrong proportions, wrong orientation, or shifted position).
- **Repeating reflections:** When extending a pattern, remember the orientation alternates with every reflection. Do not simply translate (slide) the shape — it must be flipped each time.
- **Different shape types:** Practice reflecting various shapes (pentagons, triangles, arrows, irregular polygons) to build confidence with different vertices and angles.
