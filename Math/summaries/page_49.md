## Page 49 — Measurement: Time (Measurement)

### Concepts
- Reading analogue clocks and expressing the time in words
- Reading digital watches/clocks
- Converting between analogue time (12-hour with am/pm) and 24-hour time
- Expressing time in words using phrases such as "half past", "minutes to", "minutes past", "quarter to/past"
- Understanding the relationship between "We say" (words), "Analogue time" (12-hour with am/pm), and "24-hour time"

---

### Key Rules
- **Analogue time** uses a 12-hour format with **am** (midnight to noon) and **pm** (noon to midnight).
- **24-hour time** runs from 00:00 (midnight) to 23:59; hours after 12 noon are written as 13:00, 14:00, etc.
- To convert pm analogue time to 24-hour time: add 12 to the hour (e.g., 1:50 pm = 13:50).
- To convert 24-hour time back to analogue: if the hour is 13 or greater, subtract 12 and label pm.
- "Half past" = 30 minutes past the hour; "quarter past" = 15 minutes past; "quarter to" = 15 minutes before the next hour.
- "Minutes to" means subtract those minutes from the next full hour (e.g., "10 minutes to 8" = 7:50).

### Areas to Focus On

- **"Minutes to" vs "Minutes past"**: "10 minutes to 8" means 7:50, NOT 8:10. This is a common confusion point. Practice converting "to" phrasing by subtracting from the next hour.
- **24-hour time conversion for pm times**: Always add 12 to the hour for pm times (e.g., 8:45 pm = 20:45, NOT 08:45). Several student answers on this page show errors where 12 was not added for evening/afternoon times.
- **"Half past" always means :30**: Half past 10 = 10:30, not 9:45 or 10:45. Ensure the minute hand position is understood as the 6 on the clock face.
- **am vs pm labelling**: Morning = am, afternoon/evening/night = pm. Be careful with "at night" -- times like 10:00 at night are pm (22:00 in 24-hour time).
- **Reading analogue clocks carefully**: Pay close attention to which number the hour hand is between and where the minute hand points. The hour hand moves slowly between numbers as minutes pass.
- **Practice the full cycle**: Given any one form (words, analogue, or 24-hour), practise converting to the other two forms fluently. Use a blank table and fill in all three columns from scratch.
