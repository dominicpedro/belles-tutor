## Page 41 — Reflections and Rotations of Shapes (Space & Shape / Geometry)

### Concepts
- Extending geometric patterns that use reflections and rotations
- Reflecting a shape across a line of symmetry on a dot grid
- Rotating a shape on a dot grid
- Recognising the difference between a reflection and a rotation
- Identifying what stays the same (size, shape) and what changes (orientation, direction) when reflecting or rotating
- Creating patterns by combining reflections and rotations

### Key Rules
- **Reflection (flip):** A shape is flipped over a line of symmetry. The reflected shape is a mirror image — it faces the opposite direction but remains the same size and shape.
- **Rotation (turn):** A shape is turned around a point by a certain angle. The rotated shape keeps its orientation relative to itself but changes the direction it faces.
- When reflecting, the shape reverses (like looking in a mirror). When rotating, the shape turns but does not reverse.
- In a reflection, corresponding points are the same distance from the line of reflection.
- Patterns can be built by alternating reflections and rotations of an original shape.

**Question 3:** Mary and Lerato reflect this shape. What is the same and what is different between the reflections?

A yellow arrow-like shape is shown. Two reflections are provided:
- **Mary's reflection:** The yellow shape is reflected, producing a mirror image that appears to flip horizontally (the arrow faces the opposite direction).
- **Lerato's reflection:** The yellow shape is also reflected, but across a different line of reflection, producing a mirror image that faces a different direction than Mary's.

- **Answer (written by student):** "Mary's shapes are closer and Lerato's shapes are further."
  - The key observation is that both reflections produce the same size and shape (that is what is the same), but the **line of reflection is in a different position** — Mary reflected the shape closer to the original (shorter distance from the line of reflection) while Lerato reflected it further away (greater distance from the line of reflection).
  - **What is the same:** Both are valid reflections. The reflected shapes are the same size and shape as the original. Both are mirror images.
  - **What is different:** The distance between the original shape and the reflected shape differs. Mary's reflection is closer to the original; Lerato's is further away. This means they used different lines of reflection (Mary's line of reflection was closer to the shape; Lerato's was further from the shape).

### Areas to Focus On
- **Distinguishing reflection from rotation:** A reflection flips the shape (mirror image), while a rotation turns it. Practise identifying which transformation was used by checking if the shape is reversed or just turned.
- **Using dot grids accurately:** When reflecting or rotating on a dot grid, count dots carefully to ensure the new shape is the correct size and in the correct position.
- **Line of reflection matters:** As shown in Question 3, where you place the line of reflection changes the position of the reflected image. The shape and size stay the same, but the distance from the original changes.
- **Describing transformations in words:** Be prepared to explain what you did (e.g., "I reflected the shape across a vertical line" or "I rotated the shape 90 degrees clockwise"). Use correct mathematical vocabulary: reflect, rotate, flip, turn, mirror image, line of symmetry.
- **Pattern recognition:** When extending a pattern, first identify whether the rule is reflection, rotation, or a combination, then apply it consistently.
- **Practise on dot paper:** Draw shapes on dot grids and practise reflecting them across vertical and horizontal lines, and rotating them by quarter turns, half turns, and three-quarter turns.
