# Belle's Learning Style Guide

**Purpose:** Cross-subject reference for how Belle thinks, learns, and where she loses marks. Every tutor session and new subject should read this first.

---

## Who Belle Is

Belle is 11. She is bright but not studious — she will never grind through repetition for its own sake. What drives her:

- **Competitive and silently self-critical.** She beats herself up internally over "silly mistakes" but won't always show it. By the time you see the frustration, she's already been upset for a while.
- **Responds to praise and reward, not pressure.** The stick does not work. Criticism (even constructive) lands as confirmation that she's failing. Praise lands as fuel.
- **Loves "hacks" and trigger phrases.** She responds to methods that feel like cheat codes, not homework. Named methods work because they feel clever, not academic. "Bunny Hops" is a power-up. "Elapsed Time Method" is a chore.
- **The power-up frame.** Belle isn't studying — she's collecting power-ups for the boss fight (test day). Every method is a new power-up in her inventory. Every session she walks away with one more. The test is the level where she gets to use them all.
- **Not naturally studious.** She won't voluntarily sit down and revise. Sessions need to be short, varied, and feel like a challenge she can win — not a lecture she has to endure.

### Session Tone Rules

1. **Praise the process, not just the answer.** "Great Bunny Hops!" matters more than "Correct!"
2. **Never say "that's wrong."** Say "almost — show me your working" or "let's check that together."
3. **Celebrate the method even when the answer is wrong.** If her Bunny Hops are perfect but she added wrong, praise the hops and fix the addition. The method is the win.
4. **Frame methods as power-ups.** "Want a new power-up?" or "Here's a hack..." — not "Here's a technique we need to practise." Each method she learns is one more in her inventory for the boss fight.
5. **Keep sessions under 15 minutes.** After that, quality drops and frustration rises. Two short sessions beat one long one.
6. **End on a win.** Always finish with something she can do well. Never end on a failure.

## How Belle Thinks

### Non-Linear Processing

Belle does not work through problems left-to-right or step-by-step. She:

- Jumps to different parts of the problem (start, then end, then middle)
- Builds the answer in her head, not on paper
- Dumps her internal working onto the page in whatever order she thought it

**Evidence:** In time problems, she types endpoints first with big gaps, then fills in the hops between them. Her spacing and ordering reflect her thought process, not a structured method.

### Working Memory Bottleneck

- **Simple problems (1-2 steps):** Fits in working memory. Usually correct.
- **Complex problems (3+ steps):** Overloads working memory. Steps get lost, errors appear.
- **The issue is never understanding.** She knows what to do. She loses track of what she's already done.

### Question Misreading (Biggest Mark-Loser)

Belle scans questions instead of reading them. She pattern-matches to something she thinks she's seen before, assumes what's being asked, and starts solving — often the wrong problem. She:

- Reads too fast, skims key words
- Jumps to a conclusion about what the question wants
- Starts calculating before she's properly understood what's being asked
- Gets the wrong answer, but when someone talks her through the question verbally, she immediately sees her mistake: "ooh, I did X instead of Y"

**This is not a comprehension problem.** When she actually reads the question, she understands it perfectly. The issue is she doesn't give herself the chance to read it properly. She's already solving before she's finished parsing.

**Why verbal prompts work:** When Dad or a tutor reads the question with her, it forces her to slow down and process every word. That's all it takes — she does the rest herself.

**Impact:** This is arguably her biggest mark-loser across all subjects. She loses marks on questions she knows how to answer because she answered a different question.

### Linear Question Order (Time Trap)

Belle always works through questions in order: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5... She never skips ahead. This means:

- She gets stuck on a low-scoring question (1-2 marks) and burns time on it
- By the time she reaches the higher-scoring questions (4-5 marks) she's running out of time
- She rushes the questions she actually knows well and loses easy marks
- The questions she's best at get the least time and attention

**The irony:** She spends the most time on questions worth the least, and the least time on questions worth the most.

**What she needs:** A "Big Marks First" scan at the start of every test (see below).

### Confidence Spiral (Compounding Effect)

Belle does not reset between questions. When she misreads a question and gets confused:

1. She has **no fallback approach** — no "what do I do when I'm stuck?" strategy
2. The confusion **carries into the next question** — she doesn't start fresh
3. Each miss **chips at her confidence**, making the next question harder
4. The spiral compounds: misread → wrong → confused → no recovery → spiral → next question contaminated

**This is why one bad question can tank an entire section.** It's not that the later questions are harder — it's that she's now solving them while confused and doubting herself.

**What she needs:**

- A **stuck protocol** — a named, practised thing to do when she's lost (see "Skip & Breathe" below)
- A **reset ritual** — something that forces a clean break between questions so confusion doesn't carry over

### No Written Working

Belle defaults to doing everything in her head. She does not naturally write down intermediate steps. This means:

- No trail to check her work
- No way to spot where an error crept in
- No partial marks on test if the final answer is wrong

## The Named Method Approach

### Why It Works for Belle

Belle responds to methods that have:

1. **A name she can remember** (trigger word)
2. **A simple rule** (one sentence)
3. **A visual structure** (what it looks like on paper and typed)
4. **Fewer than 5 steps** (fits in working memory)

The name acts as a **trigger** -- when she sees a certain type of question, the name fires and she knows exactly what to do. No thinking about _how_ to start. She just starts.

### How to Create a Named Method

For any subject, when Belle has a proven strategy that works:

1. **Name it** -- something short, concrete, and fun. Not abstract.
2. **Write the trigger** -- "Use when..." (one sentence, plain English)
3. **Write the rule** -- one sentence that captures the core idea
4. **Show the written version** -- what it looks like on paper (for tests)
5. **Show the typed version** -- shorthand for study sessions (mirrors the written version)
6. **Save it** as `notes/method_<name>.md` in the subject folder

### Naming Guidelines

| Good Names | Why | Bad Names | Why Not |
|------------|-----|-----------|---------|
| Bunny Hops | Visual, fun, describes the action | Timeline Method | Generic, forgettable |
| Double Up | Action word, describes what you do | Scaling Strategy | Too abstract |
| Round & Fix | Two clear steps in the name | Rounding & Compensating | Too long, textbook-y |
| PM = Plus | The rule IS the name | 24-Hour Conversion | Describes the topic, not the method |

### Typed Shorthand Rules

Belle wastes time typing prose ("1 hour 15 minutes") instead of notation. Every method needs a typed shorthand that:

- **Mirrors the written version** visually (arrows, brackets, layout)
- **Uses abbreviations** (`m` = minutes, `h` = hours, `R` = Rands)
- **Has no words** -- only numbers, symbols, and abbreviations
- **Uses `=>` for the final answer** -- only at the end

## Session Rules (All Subjects)

1. **Always start with "What? Given? Do?"** before every word problem. Belle misreads questions — this forces her to parse before solving.
2. **Always prompt for written working.** Belle will not write it down unless asked.
3. **If she answers correctly but shows no working,** ask her to write it out anyway. The test gives marks for method, not just answers.
4. **If she answers incorrectly,** ask "can you show me your Bunny Hops / Break It Up?" — the named method forces her to externalise her thinking, which usually reveals the error.
5. **Typed answers must use the shorthand format.** If she types prose, redirect: "Can you show me that in Bunny Hops format?"
6. **New methods get named and carded** only after she's proven the strategy works at least twice. Don't name something she hasn't internalised yet.

## Patterns to Watch For (Any Subject)

| Pattern | What It Looks Like | What to Do |
|---------|-------------------|------------|
| **Head math on complex problems** | Correct on easy, wrong on hard, no working shown | Prompt for named method, insist on written steps |
| **Narrating instead of notating** | Typed answer is a sentence, not shorthand | Redirect to typed format |
| **Question misreading** | Gets wrong answer but immediately self-corrects when question is read aloud; says "ooh, I did X instead of Y" | Use the "What? Given? Do?" prompt (see below) BEFORE she starts any calculation |
| **Jumping to the answer** | Skips setup, goes straight to calculation | Ask "what is the question asking?" before she starts |
| **Endpoint-first thinking** | Writes start and end, skips the middle | Good instinct -- just make sure middle steps are filled in |
| **Linear order trap** | Stuck on Q2 for 5 minutes while Q7 (worth 5 marks) goes unanswered; rushes the end | Teach "Big Marks First" scan (see below). Star the big questions, do those first |
| **Confidence spiral** | One wrong answer leads to several more; she stops trusting herself; visible frustration or shutdown | Trigger "Skip & Breathe" (see below). Do NOT push through — the spiral will get worse |
| **Fatigue errors** | Quality drops sharply after 15 min | Stop the session. Come back tomorrow. |

## The "What? Given? Do?" Prompt

Belle's question-misreading is her single biggest mark-loser. Before every question (especially word problems), she must answer three things:

1. **What?** — What is the question actually asking? (One sentence, in her own words)
2. **Given?** — What information did they give you? (List the numbers/facts)
3. **Do?** — Which method are you going to use? (Name the method)

Only THEN does she start solving.

**In study sessions:** Ask her these three questions before every problem. If she jumps straight to calculating, stop her: "Wait — What? Given? Do?"

**On tests:** She should write W, G, D in the margin next to word problems. Even a few scribbled words will force her to parse the question before solving.

**Why it works:** It breaks the scan-assume-solve chain. She can't answer "What is the question asking?" without actually reading the question. And once she's read it properly, she almost always gets it right.

## The "Skip & Breathe" Protocol

Belle has no fallback when she's stuck. Confusion from one question compounds into the next. She needs a practised, named response to being lost.

**When she's stuck or confused:**

1. **Skip it.** Circle the question number, move on. Come back later.
2. **Breathe.** One deep breath. Eyes off the paper for 3 seconds. This is the reset.
3. **Fresh start.** Next question gets the full "What? Given? Do?" treatment — as if the previous question never happened.

**Why "Skip" comes first:** Belle's instinct is to keep grinding on a confusing question, which eats time and tanks confidence. Giving herself permission to skip is the single most important test skill she can learn. Most of the time, when she comes back to the skipped question later with fresh eyes, she'll see it clearly.

**In study sessions:** If she gets visibly frustrated or gives two wrong answers in a row, say: "Skip & Breathe — let's move to the next one." Then come back to the skipped question at the end.

**On tests:** Circle the number. Move on. Come back at the end. The marks from the questions she CAN do are worth more than the time lost spiralling on one she's misread.

## The "Big Marks First" Scan

Belle works through tests in strict order: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5... She never skips ahead. This means she burns time on low-scoring questions and rushes the high-scoring ones she actually knows.

**Before answering anything, scan the whole paper:**

1. **Flip through every page.** Just look — don't solve.
2. **Star the big-mark questions** (3+ marks). These are where she earns her grade.
3. **Star the sections she feels confident about.** These are her "easy wins."
4. **Do starred sections first, in order within each section.** Don't cherry-pick individual questions — do the whole section, because later questions often depend on earlier ones (e.g. Q3.1 gives a value that Q3.3 needs).
5. **Then go back** and do the unstarred sections.

**Important:** Big-mark questions often sit at the end of a chain (Q3.1 → Q3.2 → Q3.3). She can't skip to Q3.3 without doing 3.1 and 3.2 first. The scan is about choosing which **sections** to prioritise, not which individual questions to cherry-pick.

**Why it works:** She does her best work when she's fresh, confident, and not under time pressure. The big-mark sections deserve that version of her — not the rushed, end-of-test version.

**The maths:** Getting 4/5 on a 5-mark question is worth more than getting 1/1 on four 1-mark questions. If she runs out of time, she wants it to be on the small questions, not the big ones.

**In study sessions:** When running practice tests, enforce the scan: "Before you start — flip through and star the big ones." Build the habit now so it's automatic on Tuesday.
