## Unit 1: How We Find Out About the San (pp. 90–92)

The San and Khoikhoi did not have a writing system — they never wrote anything down. So how do we know about them? There are **five sources**:

| # | Source | What it means |
|---|--------|---------------|
| 1 | **Objects** | Things they made (e.g. Khoikhoi pots with pointed bases) |
| 2 | **Stories** | Oral stories passed down by mouth (e.g. gathering and hunting songs) |
| 3 | **Rock art** | Paintings on rocks found all over South Africa |
| 4 | **Books** | Books written about them by other people |
| 5 | **Ethnography** | Studying people who live in a similar way today |

### Watch Out!

**Ethnography** is a favourite test word. It means: **studying a group of people by observing how they live, their customs and daily life.** Think: ethno = people, graphy = writing about. Scientists watch modern San people to learn about San people from long ago.

### Division of Labour

The San split up the work:

| Who | What they did |
|-----|---------------|
| **Men** | Hunted wild animals for meat |
| **Women** | Gathered plant food (roots, nuts, fruits), collected medicine plants, looked after children |

Women used **digging sticks** (made by the men) to dig up roots. One root could take 20 minutes to dig up! Children were carried everywhere and breastfed until about age four.

## Unit 2: The San Hunter-Gatherer Society (pp. 93–95)

### Key Facts

- The San were the **first people** to live in South Africa
- They lived during the **Stone Age** — a time when people made tools from stone and bone
- They were called **hunter-gatherers** because they hunted animals and gathered plant food
- They did NOT grow crops or keep animals — they got everything from the environment
- They caused **no damage** to the environment

### Nomadic Life

The San were **nomadic** — they moved from place to place looking for food and water. Because they moved so much:
- They lived in **small groups or families**
- They had **very few belongings** (you can't carry much when you're always moving)
- They **shared** everything — this kept everyone equal and stopped jealousy

### Water Storage

In the dry season, water was hard to find. The San:
- Dug holes in the sand to find water
- Squeezed and scraped roots for moisture
- Stored water in **ostrich egg shells** (sealed with a grass or clay plug)

Ostrich egg shells worked because they are very hard and can be buried underground for a long time.

## Unit 3: Medicine from Plants (p. 96)

The San had expert knowledge of which plants could treat which illnesses. **Women** went out in groups to collect plants.

| Plant | What it does |
|-------|-------------|
| **Buchu** | Cures stomach aches, headaches, and toothaches (the all-purpose medicine) |
| **Hoodia** | Stops hunger and thirst — hunters chewed it while hunting so they could keep going |

### Snake Bite Treatment

If someone was bitten by a snake, the San smeared the **milky sap** from a succulent plant onto the bite.

### Watch Out!

Know all **three** treatments: buchu (everyday aches), hoodia (hunter's plant), and plant sap for snake bites.

## Unit 4: The Hunt and the Bow and Arrow (pp. 97–98)

### How They Made the Bow and Arrow

| Part | Made from |
|------|-----------|
| **Bow** | A straight, tough, elastic branch |
| **Bow string** | Rolled strips from the back muscles of a **gemsbok** |
| **Arrow point** | Stone or bone |
| **Poison** | From a small **beetle** (beetle larvae) — the most deadly of all San poisons |

The poison was placed **behind** the arrow point (not on the tip) — so the hunter wouldn't accidentally poison himself if he got scratched.

### Watch Out!

The poison came from a **beetle**, not a snake or a plant. This is a detail the test might ask about.

### Key Vocabulary

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Stamina** | The ability to keep going for a long time without getting tired |
| **Tracking** | Following animal footprints and signs to find them |
| **Prey** | An animal that is hunted for food (NOT "pray" = talking to God!) |
| **Game** | Wild animals, birds, or fish hunted for food |

### The Hunt Process (Step by Step)

1. Hunters **tracked** the animal by following its footprints
2. They followed the herd for **many days** (needed stamina!)
3. They got close enough to **shoot** with a bow and arrow
4. The poison worked **slowly** — it didn't kill the animal straight away
5. They kept following the animal until it **collapsed**
6. They cut out the part with the arrow (threw it away because of poison)
7. They used **spears** to finish killing the animal
8. They used **sharp stones** to **skin** the animal
9. They cut up the meat and brought it back to camp
10. The whole community had a **feast** — singing and dancing around the fire

### Watch Out!

This is a likely **paragraph question**: "Explain the skills the San needed to hunt." Include: tracking, stamina, patience (poison works slowly), knowledge of direction and environment, and the community feast at the end.

## Unit 6: San Rock Art (pp. 101–102)

### What is an Archaeologist?

An **archaeologist** is a person who studies objects from long ago to find out how people lived. They dig up and examine old things people left behind.

### The Rock Art

- San rock art is found all over South Africa — it can be up to **20 000 years old**
- There are roughly **20 000** rock art sites in South Africa
- The paintings are NOT just everyday drawings — they contain **religious symbols and meanings**
- It's difficult to work out what the paintings mean because **the artists died long before archaeologists began studying them**

### Colours and Paint Materials

| Colour | How they made it |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Red** | Natural materials + blood as binder |
| **Yellow** | Natural materials |
| **White** | White clay mixed with water |
| **Black** | Natural materials |

They used **blood** and **egg white** to bind (hold) the colours together.

### Painting Tools

| Tool | Used for |
|------|----------|
| **Sticks** | Painting the body of the animal |
| **Fingers** | Painting fine details |
| **Hollow reed** | Spraying paint onto the rock (like a straw — blow through it!) |

## Unit 7: SA Coat of Arms and the Linton Panel (pp. 103–104)

### Key Vocabulary

| Word | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Identity** | Who you are; what makes you or a group special |
| **Motto** | A short sentence or phrase that represents what you believe in |
| **Coat of arms** | Pictures with a special meaning on a shield — an official symbol of a country |

### Parts of the Coat of Arms

| Part | Where |
|------|-------|
| Rising sun | Top |
| Protea | Upper sides |
| Knobkierie | Left side |
| Shield | Centre |
| Spear | Right side |
| Two San figures | Inside the shield |
| Elephant tusks | Lower sides |
| Ears of wheat | Lower area |
| Motto | Bottom |

### The Motto

- Written in **San language**: **!ke e: /xarra //ke**
- Means: **"People who are different join together"**
- Written in a San language because the San were the **first people** in South Africa — it honours them

### The Linton Panel

- A famous San rock art painting found on a farm called **Linton** in the **Eastern Cape**
- It was moved to the **South African Museum in Cape Town** to protect it from weather and damage
- The **two human figures** on the coat of arms come from the Linton Panel
- The figure is shown **twice** — greeting each other — to represent **unity**
- The two figures stand for **unity** and **happiness**

### Watch Out!

Know the connection: Linton Panel (rock art) → figures on coat of arms → motto about unity → all linking to the San as the first people of South Africa.
