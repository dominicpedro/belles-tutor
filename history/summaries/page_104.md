## Key Facts

- A large photograph of the **Linton Panel** is shown on this page — it is a piece of rock with San paintings on it, displayed in a museum (on a red surface/stand)
- The rock art shows human figures and animals painted in red, brown, and other natural colours
- The figures from this panel are the ones used on the South African coat of arms
- Images from the Linton Panel are used in the South African coat of arms

### Contextual note (partially visible at top of page)

## Activities

### Activity 9: Understanding the Coat of Arms

Students look at the photograph of the Linton Panel (page 104) and the coat of arms (page 103), then answer:

1. **Find drawings of people on the Linton Panel. Compare the people on the coat of arms with people on the Linton Panel.**
   - Expected: Students should identify the two human figures on the coat of arms and find similar figures in the Linton Panel rock art; they should note that the coat of arms figures are based on the Linton Panel figures

2. **You will notice that the person on the coat of arms has been used twice so that the two figures look as if they are greeting each other. Why do you think they are shown like this?**
   - Expected: The two figures greeting each other represent **unity** — people coming together; this connects to the motto "People who are different join together"

3. **Choose two words from the options below to describe what the two figures stand for:**
   - a. Unity
   - b. Strength
   - c. Happiness
   - d. Sadness
   - **Expected answer: a. Unity and c. Happiness** (the figures greeting each other symbolise people uniting and the joy of coming together)

---

## Study Focus

1. Be able to recognise the Linton Panel and know it is the source of the figures on the coat of arms
2. Understand WHY the figure is shown twice: to show two people **greeting each other**, symbolising **unity**
3. Know that the figures represent **unity** (links to the motto "People who are different join together")
4. Be able to compare the Linton Panel rock art with the coat of arms and explain the connection
5. Remember that San rock art can be up to **20 000 years old**
